# The Month of the Ashen Dreaming

Prince Caeris Thorne did not sleep soundly in the Month of the Ashen Dreaming. Three nights running he woke with the smell of burning pitch in his throat and the image of Tidefall's harbor split open by firelight — the Admiral's flagship cracking at the keel, harbor-light rising from the wreckage in a shape no sailor would afterward describe. He sent no more letters through the Gnome routes. He stood instead outside his father's sealed chamber door, speaking to the wood in a low voice, as though King Roderic Thorne II might yet answer him, as though the sound of two calm voices within and the scratch of something being signed were signs of life rather than its quiet, final accounting. The steward had stopped counting guard rotations. He had started writing a will. In the courts below, minor lords had begun composing their own correspondence — addressed not to the throne, but to the Admiral — and the Gloomspire Syndicate, who learned of the Prince's screaming through a household servant who could not keep a secret, sat in their counting rooms and turned the knowledge over like a coin of uncertain denomination, wondering who would pay most dearly to possess it.

Beneath the Lostfeld mountains, the hammering changed. It had counted before — patient, rhythmic, a thing that measured the distance between now and some approaching hour. It counted no longer. It called. Kessa Stonebraid woke in her confinement room with bloody fingertips and no memory of rising, and found the walls around her covered in the same symbols that traced themselves across her neck, scratched into stone by her own sleeping hand as though the Entity below had simply reached up through her bones and written its intentions on the world. She asked Thane Ulric Ironmaul, very quietly, not to seal the door entirely. Ironmaul, who had not stood from his chair in days, stood. The Monastery of Druids had finally answered his missive — the first outside correspondence the Conclave had initiated in six years — and whatever the Grand Druid had written in return sent Ironmaul to his desk in a fury, sleepless, writing back before the ink of the first letter had fully dried. He did not yet know what the Conclave knew: that the language the Grand Druid read aloud each night from the pre-Accord book, haltingly, with growing comprehension, was the same language being hammered upward through the roots of Lostfeld. The Gnome cartographer who had been transcribing the upward-falling snow letters near the Frostvale ridge had vanished, leaving only his final note behind — *It is not a language. It is a name. It is very long* — and the gate at the base of the mountain breathed warmth like a forge running in the deep, melting every snowflake within fifty feet before it could land, open and patient and utterly unhurried.

Sovereign Elowen Silverleaf had ridden to the ridge herself and said nothing when she arrived, only turned her war-bands away from the Shadow Court positions and set them watching Lostfeld instead. No ceasefire had been announced. No explanation had been given to the soldiers, who held their discipline by the authority of her bearing alone and asked no questions they did not expect to have answered. The Shadow Court had not been informed, and Velorn clan's resentment had grown loud enough now to be spoken aloud in council — loud enough, too, to dispatch a quiet envoy to the ridge, not to Elowen, but to the Arkenor lieutenant, to hear what he had said to her before she changed everything. Lythara did not know. On the open sea, Pirate King Rowen anchored his fleet in deep water off the sinking island and went ashore alone each dawn in a small boat, returning each dusk without speaking. On the third visit he brought back a wrapped object no larger than a hand — a key, his captains chose to believe, to a lock not seen above water in living memory — and locked it in his personal chest and did not touch it again, while the eastern cliffs of the island settled two spans lower into the sea than they had stood the season before, and the descent, by every sailor's reckoning, was accelerating.
