# The Twelfth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

Caeris Thorne woke before the sun had fully claimed the sky, and for a long moment he lay still with his eyes open, watching the dream dissolve the way smoke dissolves — slowly, and against his wishes. He had sat upon the throne of Tidefall. The Admiral's seat beside him had been empty. He told no one of this. Not his steward, not the Seer who still walked the palace grounds like a man reading weather in stone. Instead, Caeris dressed in silence, sent for his garrison commander before the morning meal had cooled, and issued an order he framed as routine: every soldier in the palace district of the Twin Cities to be accounted for by nightfall, every post logged, every face named. The commander complied without question, which was precisely what Caeris needed and precisely what damned him — because the Seer, taking his second morning walk along the colonnaded edge of the inner yard, watched the soldiers being counted and understood the shape of it without understanding the cause. A man who has signed something, the Seer wrote in the private note he had not yet decided whether to send, *prepares the ground around it before he announces what he has done.*

Far to the south, in Tidefall's harbor district where the salt-wind moved through counting houses and admiralty chambers alike, the Admiral pressed his seal into wax with the satisfaction of a man who believes he has outmaneuvered fortune. The harbor-treaty was complete. Two witnesses from his council stood at the table's edge. A notification rider was already saddled for the Twin Cities road. What the Admiral did not know — what no one in that sunlit chamber knew — was that buried within the treaty's retainer clauses, the Dark Council of Dur Khadur had secured autonomous authorization to deploy their advance unit the moment succession uncertainty was formally declared by *anyone*, from *any* quarter, for *any* reason. The Admiral had signed a trigger he did not control. At the coastal staging area, Dur Khadur's forward soldiers received word of the countersignature before the ink on the admiralty copy had fully dried, and they began their preparations with the quiet efficiency of men who have been waiting a long time and are very good at it.

It was a servant's loose tongue that had undone the pre-dawn secrecy of Day Eleven, and by midmorning of the twelfth day the assembled inference had found its buyer. Three silver changed hands in a place no record would note, and by evening the Gnome Syndicate held the most complete account of the Twin Cities succession that existed anywhere in the known world — the two lords, the private chamber, the candle burned before dawn, the Seer sleeping nearby when the proclamation was signed. They were in no hurry. Patience was the Syndicate's oldest weapon, and they sharpened it now with visible pleasure, weighing three futures against one another: sell to Tidefall and let the Admiral learn before his court, sell to Caeris Thorne and let the dreaming lord know exactly who else knew, or hold the intelligence like a millstone above still water and wait for the moment when its fall would cause the most irreversible ripple. Meanwhile, in a highland camp four hours south of Lostfeld's outer gate, Grand Druid Varek reopened the sealed iron case he had carried from the Monastery of Druids and read its contents alone for two hours in the firelight before closing it again, his expression offering nothing to the dark. In the southern passes, a blue-corridor dream had passed from a Lostfeld soldier's lips to a Vilefin goblin scout's ears, and something old and patient beneath the earth extended itself, quietly, into the wider world for the first time — indifferent to stone walls, indifferent to isolation orders, indifferent to the name Ironmaul whispered to himself as he waited for morning and counted the hours that remained before Varek arrived.
