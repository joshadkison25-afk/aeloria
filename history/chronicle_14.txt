# The Fourteenth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

Before the second bell had finished its echo across the Twin Cities harbor, the Seer was already gone — a solitary figure pressing south along the road with his writing materials bundled against his coat and no name given at the gate but the direction itself. He did not know what waited at the end of that road. He knew only that the eclipse had drawn lines between things that had not previously touched one another, and that those lines all converged somewhere near the sea-smell of Tidefall. He walked as a man walks who trusts the ache in his feet more than the logic in his head. Behind him, the city he left did not pause. Caeris Thorne sat at his private desk reading a letter he had not asked for — an anonymous thing, unmarked, delivered through a servant whose name he would not be able to confirm by nightfall — and the letter told him, with the cold economy of someone who already held every card, that others knew. Not what they knew. Only that they did. He read it twice. Then he set it beside the Gnome offer's outer seal, which he had broken but not gone further, and he called his secretary in and asked who else in the palace had received sealed correspondence in the last two days. He had stopped moving toward the proclamation and begun moving toward a wall, and the difference between those two directions was the difference between a man who shapes events and a man who is shaped by them.

In Tidefall, the Admiral had not spoken since the council dismissed. His prophet had delivered the eclipse reading with evident care — *what emerges from the darkness is clarified, not diminished; a leader who acts in the eclipse's wake acts with the sun behind him* — and the Admiral had received this with eyes that did not focus on anything in the room. Those who sat near him later said it was not grief, not anger. It was the look of a man who had seen something the night before that the morning could not yet touch. He had sent them all away before the reading was properly finished, and by mid-morning he sat alone behind the locked door of his private study with the Dur Khadur message in one hand and the prophet's folded note in the other, the two documents side by side like an argument he had not been asked to adjudicate but could no longer avoid. The dream had not left him. He had not named it to anyone — not the chamberlain who found him shaking, not the council who watched his silence — but the gnome was still in the room with him, the impossible anatomy of it, the invitation it had extended in a voice that sounded like it already knew he would accept. The Dur Khadur pressure arrived at his chamberlain's hand before noon: *uncertainty invites worse solutions than any declaration.* He had read it once. He had not put it down.

Beneath Lostfeld, Grand Druid Varek had now been below the sealed level for more than a full day, and the silence that rose from that depth was not the silence of a man at work. Thane Ironmaul had given the order for a second iron door in a quiet voice, and the garrison smith had begun the work without asking why, because the kind of man who builds walls for a living understands when a wall is needed even before the reason has been spoken aloud. At the threshold above, Kessa Stonebraid had not moved. The eclipse news sat in its folded parchment where it had been left, uncollected. Somewhere on the road south, a fast rider from Stonebreak carried a sealed instruction from the Grand Druid Council — *seal it again, do not let it breathe a second time* — and the rider was pushing hard, but the distance between an instruction and the moment it becomes too late is a distance that has no reliable measure, and the world, as it always does, continued on without asking anyone's permission.
