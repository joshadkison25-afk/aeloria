# The Twentieth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

At dawn in the sealed garrison of Lostfeld, Grand Druid Varek spoke a name into the cold air as though he had always known it — *Aldric* — and the word landed among the dwarves like a stone dropped into still water. No one had spoken that name in his presence. No letter had been read aloud, no conversation had been carelessly had within earshot of the man who sat cross-legged before the iron case each night and made sounds that were not distress and not prayer but something older than either. Thane Ironmaul asked how he knew, and Varek only looked at the case. When Kessa Stonebraid pressed her rune-warden's fingers to the iron that morning she found what the night had left behind: three spiral etchings, precise and deliberate, formed not from without but from within the metal itself, as though something sealed inside had grown patient enough to learn to write. Ashveil recognized them. She stood in the corner of the chamber with the stillness of a woman choosing very carefully what her face would do, and she said nothing — because what the spirals meant she had read once in a text called the *Concordance of Sealed Thresholds*, a pre-institutional Monastery record so restricted it should not have existed anywhere in Lostfeld, should not have existed in any mind that had not been shown it deliberately. She sealed that knowledge behind her teeth and walked to the outer yard while Ironmaul convened his private session with the rune-wardens and had rubbings made of every mark before they could change further. He excluded her. She let him. By midmorning she had paid a Lostfeld civilian to carry a second letter south — not through the Gnome relay, which was too slow and perhaps too open — addressed to Warden Aldric in Stonebreak, marked urgent and eyes-only, bearing four words of intelligence she had shared with no one: *the case is writing*.

Far to the southwest, in the Twin Cities, Caeris Thorne moved through his day like a man navigating a burning building by memory alone. He submitted the proclamation text to the High Cleric before the morning bells and dispatched three letters by midday — a conditional acceptance to Glenhaven's Elowen, a stand-down acknowledgment to the Dark Council of Dur Khadur, a reassurance to the anxious Lord Halveth — each one a calculated stone placed in a wall he was building faster than he fully understood. Elowen received his acceptance sooner than she had anticipated, and the speed of it told her everything she needed to know about the pressure he was under; she sent her two standby Wayfarers across the Twin Cities border without modification, embedding them as observers in a succession crisis that had not yet been announced. By late afternoon, Farrock's garrison commander had added his own weight to the pile — a formal written inquiry about unusual activity in the northern corridor, routed to the regent-designate's office, where it arrived and was noted and was placed beneath everything else. Caeris wrote a marginal note in his own hand — *correlate with Dur Khadur position?* — and set it aside for tomorrow, unaware that the unusual activity in question was a man who had begun to speak the names of people he should not know, and an iron box that had learned, in the dark, to leave marks.

And in Tidefall, the council had voted — narrowly, with fracture visible in the count — that a prophet standing at a closed door and speaking for twenty minutes into silence, then emerging to say only *he is present*, did not constitute a written statement under the plain language of the incapacitation protocol. The formal deadline had been posted on the Admiral's door before evening, ink on paper nailed to wood, as though the institution believed a man who had not opened his door in two days might be moved by the ceremony of it. One day remained. On the desk inside the admiralty hall, unread and undisturbed, lay the Dur Khadur dispatch and Caeris's Hollow Accord letter, waiting for whoever would eventually sit in that chair and open them — and somewhere in Tidefall's harbor district a grey-cloaked figure had left a package at an inn three days before all of this began, and given no name, and the Vampire operative had ruled out every institutional hand that might have sent it, which meant the hand that had was unknown, which meant it was something else entirely, something that had not yet shown its purpose, moving quietly beneath the surface of a world that was holding its breath on every front, waiting for the twentieth day to end and the twenty-first to arrive with whatever it intended to break open first.
