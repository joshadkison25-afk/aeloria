# The Eleventh Day of the Ashen Dreaming

Before the first grey light had touched the spires of the Twin Cities, Caeris Thorne pressed his seal into wax that had not yet cooled, and the thing was done. Two lords stood witness in the candlelit chamber — men who owed him debts older than the king's illness — and when they departed before sunrise, moving separately through corridors that were not yet awake, Caeris pinched the candle's flame between his fingers and stood alone in the dark with the proclamation drying before him. He was, by law, regent. No trumpet had sounded. No herald had spoken. The Seer slept in the side chamber, unaware that the words he had carried across half the world had already done their work, and in the palace laundry a steward who noticed things — the unusual hour, the candle stub, the careful way two lords do not look at one another when they believe no one is watching — filed the fragments away without yet understanding their shape. Elsewhere in the same palace, a Gnome agent was listening to that steward describe his morning, and the fragments were becoming something dangerous.

Far to the northeast, across the grey chop of open water, Pirate King Rowen stood on the deck of his flagship in the pale dawn and summoned his three senior captains to a meeting that lasted exactly twenty minutes. He gave them enough — *a chamber below the throne room, a record written before the accord* — and no more. Sealed orders changed hands. The fleet's course bent eastward by several quiet degrees, and the crew, who had watched their king seal himself inside his cabin for two days, understood only that he had emerged purposeful rather than broken, which was enough to put their hands back on the ropes. What Rowen had read in that iron-doored chamber beneath the sinking island was not a thing he would speak in open air, but the course adjustment told its own story to anyone who knew the eastern lanes well enough to read the destination in the heading.

In Lostfeld, under the mountain, Thane Ulric Ironmaul had spent the morning in a chair beside Kessa Stonebraid, watching her breathe. Three of his garrison soldiers had been separated before dawn — placed in different sectors, forbidden from speaking, their offense a shared dream they could not have planned: a corridor descending without end, lit by cold blue light, the walls close and the bottom never arriving. Ironmaul had no brick to seal a dream behind. He had given the order with the face of a man who knew it would not hold, and then he had returned to Kessa, and he had said her name, and she had blinked once — slowly, as if from a great distance — and he had not left her side since. Two ticks north, Grand Druid Varek moved through the Gnome mountain passes with a sealed iron case no subordinate had been permitted to examine, paying his tolls without negotiation, moving as fast as the road allowed. The Gnomes who watched him transit noted the case, noted the speed, noted the absence of messages sent ahead, and began to consider what that combination of details was worth, and to whom.
