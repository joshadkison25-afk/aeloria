# The Month of the Iron Threshold

In the third hour past midnight, beneath the roots of Lostfeld, the sealed mine-gate opened. No hand had touched it. No mechanism had fired. It swung inward on its ancient hinges as though answering a knock that no one else had heard, and from the darkness beyond came not the deep vibration that had tormented the mountain's bones for months prior — that familiar, terrible trembling had ceased entirely — but a voice. One word, in a tongue older than dwarven script, older perhaps than the mountain itself. Kessa Stonebraid stood close enough to receive it. The symbols carved into the skin of her neck, which had crept steadily outward through previous weeks like frost finding the edges of glass, went suddenly still — and began to pulse, slow and rhythmic, in time with something far below. Thane Ulric Ironmaul ordered the gate barred with iron. The bar bent outward within the hour, deliberate as a hand brushing aside a curtain, and the gate has stood open since. Those who look into it report only darkness and the quality of silence that follows a held breath. Three foremen have vanished from their posts without a word of resignation or farewell. The word desertion has not yet been spoken aloud in Lostfeld. It will be. Ironmaul, a man who has held the mountain's trust for thirty years, now sits at his writing table with the look of a man composing wills — his sealed missives dispatched to the Gloomspire Syndicate, to the Monastery of Druids in Stonebreak, and to a third recipient whose name no living soul in Lostfeld has spoken in a generation. The courier who carried that third letter has not returned. Whatever the Thane fears, he has decided he cannot face it alone, and perhaps cannot face it at all.

To the east and west, the surface world moved with the particular urgency of those who do not yet know what stirs beneath them. In Faerwood, Lythara convened Clan Arkenor's war-council in the hours before dawn, absent both Velorn and Nythariel, the agenda unrecorded by any hand present. By morning light, Arkenor scouts were threading eastward through the ridge paths toward Frostvale — paths that Sovereign Elowen Silverleaf's war-bands now occupied in careful, watchful silence. Elowen did not order engagement. She sent instead a single rider back toward Frostvale carrying no written word, only a spoken message — its contents known to the rider and to whomever it was meant to reach, and to no one else. Above the eastern ridge, the drifting moon-shadow that had wandered Faerwood's skies for weeks came to rest precisely over the point where Arkenor scouts and Elven war-bands now converged, and it did not move again. In Frostvale itself, Prince Caeris Thorne received the letter from his brother Aldric, read it once, and fed it to the fire. Then he requested a formal audience with the Frostvale war-council — the first he has sought since his arrival — and began quietly asking which mountain passes the Gloomspire Gnomes did not control. What Aldric's letter contained is ashes now, but whispers from those who trade in such things suggest it was not a threat but an offer: Dur Khadur backing for Caeris's claim to the Twin Cities throne, in exchange for a single concession left unnamed. The snow above Frostvale fell upward again that week, and those who watched it said the flakes formed letters — in an alphabet no scholar present could name — before dissolving into nothing.

In the Twin Cities, King Roderic Thorne II signed the ultimatum, and his hand shook as he did it. The thirty-day clock to Tidefall began its count before the ink had dried, and within hours the Admiral's counter-letter arrived at the harbor — measured, unhurried, faintly contemptuous: the ships would not move, but Roderic was welcome to negotiate aboard the flagship, in the Admiral's water, on the Admiral's terms. Roderic retreated to his private library and cancelled all audiences for three days, leaving his court to govern itself in the manner of a body still moving after the head has gone quiet. Far south in the Dreadwind Islands, Pirate King Rowen had not moved from the table where his depth-charts lay since his mapping ships returned. The charts described something beneath the outer Tidefall lanes — a structure, submerged and vast, geometric in its precision, built by hands that had not touched those waters in an age beyond reckoning. He had refused every captain who asked to see them. He had eaten nothing. He sat with the knowledge the way a man sits with a weapon he has not yet decided whether to draw, sell, or bury — because his instruments, he alone knew, had recorded not one structure beneath those lanes, but two. The harbor water of Eldoria glowed faintly at night near the hulls of the three Tidefall warships, cold and luminescent, and the fishermen had stopped asking why.
