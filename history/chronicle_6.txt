# The Month of the Pale Reckoning

The Frostvale ridge had seen a thousand small violences, but it had never seen what it saw on the morning the Arkenor lieutenant crested the Pale narrows with his hands open and his sword still sheathed. Elowen Silverleaf's war-bands had their arrows nocked before he finished his first step onto Elven ground, and yet no order came to loose. He did not speak to the soldiers. He asked, by name, for the Sovereign — and something in the asking made the captains step aside rather than answer. What passed between him and Elowen in the hour that followed, no witness survived to describe in full. She dismissed them one by one as the message unfolded, until she stood alone with him in the cold and the upward-falling snow, and those waiting outside said they saw, through the gap in the tent canvas, the precise moment her face changed — not with fear, and not with triumph, but with something older and harder to name, the expression of a woman who has just learned that the war she has been fighting is not the war that matters. She gave new orders to the ridge war-bands before nightfall. The couriers who carried the second instruction have not repeated it aloud to anyone.

Beneath Lostfeld, something patient had finally grown tired of waiting. The hammering began at midday — not the hammering of tools or siege, but of intention, deliberate and metered, climbing through the stone in a rhythm that scholars of the Druidic Conclave, had any been present, would have recognized as the ceremonial pulse of pre-Accord binding rites, a cadence used in ceremonies not practiced since before most living things drew breath. It was counting. Kessa Stonebraid heard it and walked toward the open gate without a word, without a coat, without hesitation, and the two soldiers who caught her arms said afterward that she did not fight them — she simply continued moving, slowly, as though they were not there, and she was smiling with the particular serenity of someone who has finally heard the name they have waited their whole life to be called. The symbols on her neck were glowing through the cloth by the time Thane Ulric Ironmaul had her confined to quarters. He has not left the corridor outside her door since. He sleeps in his chair. He has stopped eating with any regularity. He is waiting for something to arrive from the missives he has sent into the world beyond the mountain — something, anything — before the counting reaches whatever it is counting toward.

Elsewhere, the world moved in the register of sealed doors and silent men. In the Twin Cities, King Roderic Thorne II emerged from four days of solitude only to pull his estranged brother Ser Aldric Thorne into a private chamber from which neither has since emerged, leaving a court without edicts, without appointments, without answers, while across the harbor the Tidefall Admiral sat aboard his flagship like a man already holding the deed to a house whose occupants do not yet know they are leaving — and he had begun to entertain Dur Khadur envoys at his table, which was either confidence or the announcement of something worse. At Stonebreak, the Grand Druid unsealed a chamber no living member of the Conclave had ever entered, and what they found inside — a figure seated upright, preserved against all decay, robed in an era before the Hollow Accord, hands folded over a closed book — had given him the bearing of a man who now knows precisely what he is walking toward, even if he cannot yet say whether walking toward it will save anything at all. And on the Dreadwind Islands, Rowen had finally eaten, had finally spoken, and had sent his three senior captains out of his war-room wearing the faces of men who have been told they are already dead and have decided, on reflection, to be useful about it. His fleet was not moving toward Tidefall. It was moving toward the sinking island. He had sent one message ahead of it, to a recipient no one in his fleet could name — though Rowen himself had been to that island once before, under a name none of them knew, in a life he had not yet chosen to explain.
