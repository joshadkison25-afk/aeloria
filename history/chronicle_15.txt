# The Fifteenth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

Before the garrison fires had burned down to their second coaling, Grand Druid Varek walked out of the sealed level and back into the world of breathing men. He carried the iron case at his side, and it was sealed, and he said nothing. Kessa Stonebraid had not moved from her post through the long night — had held her position with the particular stillness of someone who has run out of prayers but not yet run out of duty — and Varek passed her without slowing, without acknowledgment, without a word spoken or a glance offered that she could later describe as recognition. What she could describe, and what she would not stop describing to herself in the hours that followed, was his eyes. Not vacant. Not afraid. Something had been added to them rather than taken away, and whatever it was had no name in the language she had grown up speaking beneath Lostfeld's stone. He walked into the garrison and asked for water. That was all. Thane Ironmaul came down before the question had been answered, dressed without ceremony, and found Varek seated at the table with the iron case on the floor beside his boot, upright, sealed, patient as a grave marker. Ironmaul sat across from him and the two men regarded each other in silence for a long time, and then Ironmaul sent Kessa outside and posted guards at both doors, and the second iron door — half-framed in the outer yard, visible from the road if one knew to look — was quietly left unfinished, its iron frame standing in the cold morning air like a question that had lost its urgency.

In the Twin Cities, a different kind of threshold was being crossed, though it wore the face of an old man reaching for a pen. Caeris Thorne had not visited his father's chambers in three days, but the Gnome inner document had dissolved whatever paralysis had kept him away. He entered with the document folded against his coat and spoke for twenty minutes to King Roderic Thorne the Second, who sat by the window in the pale afternoon light with the intermittent lucidity of a man whose mind came and went like tide. The attending physician, who remained in the corner with the particular practiced invisibility of palace staff, would later remember that Caeris asked one question at the end — the kind of question, the physician would say, that a man asks when the asking is itself the answer — and that Roderic, without speaking, reached for his pen. What was written, or whether the ink touched paper before the light failed, was not yet known to anyone outside that room. It was not known to the Nythariel shadow-envoy who arrived in the city's outer district before dusk and took lodging in a merchant house and received Queen Lythara's urgent instruction by Shadow Road not two hours later: *do not wait for morning, seek Caeris tonight.* It was not known to the Vampire operative who had already sent word south along the same shadow paths — *the old king's hand may move before the sun sets, position accordingly.* And it was known to none of them — not envoy, not operative, not Caeris himself — that eight miles from the harbor approach, a Dur Khadur advance unit moved through the late afternoon under reduced armor and no banners, four miles gained since the sealed declaration reached their commander's hands before noon, the Admiral of Tidefall not having been seen since the courier departed, his prophet standing twice at a locked study door and receiving no answer either time, the council of Tidefall still unaware that their city's most powerful man had whispered a foreign army into motion and then gone silent behind oak and iron.

Far from both cities, on the open water where the cold deepened and the coastal ruin showed its broken tower above the tide line, Rowen of the Dreadwind Islands had anchored his fleet and descended himself in a shallow-draft rowing boat with his navigator and one trusted crew, studying the submerged stonework that extended further beneath the surface than the ruined tower's footprint suggested it should. He had said nothing to the rest of the fleet about what the ancient document had pointed him toward, and the senior crew watched from the deck in the way of men who have learned that questions to Rowen arrive either too early or too late and rarely on time. On the road to Lostfeld, the Vilefin tinker-informant noted in his professional, unhurried way: no supply carts for two full days, one additional garrison post at the outer gate, and in the yard, an iron frame standing unattended and unfinished in the cold — significant enough to write down, he judged, though he did not yet know what it meant. A day's ride behind him, the Monastery instruction rider was pressing hard through the dark on roads that led toward a man who had already returned from the place the rider's orders had been written to prevent him reaching. The instructions would arrive tomorrow. They would arrive, as so many instructions do, precisely one threshold too late.
