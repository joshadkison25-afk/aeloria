# The Eighth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

The Seer left Stonebreak at first light, when the sky was the color of old iron and the road north offered nothing but stone and silence. He carried no companion, no written word, only his staff and the phrase that had been pressed into him like a thumbprint in wet clay — *death walks the land* — and he walked with the particular unhurried certainty of a man who knows his arrival will change nothing and is going anyway. Somewhere behind him, moving through the same grey morning with considerably less purpose and considerably more patience, a shadow-agent of the Gloomspire Syndicate matched his pace on the ridge above the road, watching the set of his shoulders, the weight of what he carried, counting the hours until whatever the Seer knew could be sold to someone who needed it more than he did. The world did not pause. The Twin Cities did not hold its breath. Caeris Thorne stood his vigil at a sealed chamber door and knew nothing of the man walking toward him through the mountain passes, and the Seer knew nothing of the regency proclamation hidden beneath the prince's floorboards, written in a hand that had not stopped shaking since it set down the quill.

Beneath Lostfeld, the hammering had changed again. Those who remained at their posts — fewer each dawn, three more gone before sunrise, their boot-prints fading into the frost of the northern approach — described it no longer as percussion but as *intention*, as though whatever moved below the stone had learned that someone above was finally listening and had adjusted its address accordingly. Thane Ulric Ironmaul had not slept in two days. He sat at his correspondence table with the hammering's rhythm transcribed in musical notation before him, his hands still trembling from the effort of rendering something inhuman into the only language he knew, and sent it north to the Monastery of Druids with a seal pressed so hard the wax cracked at its edge. In the Grand Druid's inner chamber, an emergency Conclave had already gathered around the pre-Accord book, and the recognition that flickered through the assembled scholars when Ironmaul's earlier descriptions were read aloud was the kind of recognition that precedes terror — the slow understanding that the language they had been studying was not dead, had never been dead, and was even now being spoken upward through six hundred feet of mountain at a girl who had stopped eating and whose palms, Ironmaul noted without writing it in the missive, had begun to show the same symbols that ringed her neck.

At the Frostvale ridge, Sovereign Elowen Silverleaf had solved the problem of the Velorn envoy the way she solved most problems — quietly, without declaration, and in a manner that left every option open and none of them comfortable. The man sat in a tent with warm food and cold silence, separated from the Arkenor lieutenant by a distance Elowen had personally measured, and she stood outside both tents in the wind and did not yet know what she would say to either of them. To the south, aboard a neutral merchant vessel anchored in waters that belonged technically to no one, Tidefall's Admiral was preparing to receive two senior lords of the Twin Cities in a meeting that had no official name and would be recorded in no court ledger, while Dur Khadur envoys sat in his flagship's lower cabin and drafted the language of a mercenary consultancy that would give whatever came next its necessary teeth. The Vampires moved through it all like water through stone — one of them had already found its minor lord, already leaned close in a torchlit corridor to offer the kind of counsel that asks for very little now and remembers everything later. And in the deep anchorage of the Dreadwind Islands, Pirate King Rowen sat on the deck above a submerged iron door and held his key against his palm and counted the hand-widths the island had yet to give him, the arithmetic of salvage and ruin running behind his eyes in the dark.
