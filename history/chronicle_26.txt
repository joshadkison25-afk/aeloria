On the twenty-sixth day of the Month of the Ashen Dreaming, the archivists of Lostfeld brought their findings to Thane Ulric Ironmaul before the garrison fires had been fully stoked against the morning cold. Three rubbing-copies, pressed in the small hours from bore-survey stone older than the fortress itself, were laid upon his table without ceremony. The notation cluster was the same across all three — two from the deepest eastern shafts, one from a collapsed western gallery sealed in the third generation and touched by no living hand since — and the archivists translated it the same way each time: *the one who receives weight from below.* The Listener Below had not arrived with Grand Druid Varek. It had not been carried in doctrine or bound in Monastery ritual. It had been here, patient and present, for two centuries before the first Dwarven hammer struck stone to found Lostfeld above it. Ironmaul did not speak for a long time after the archivists withdrew. When he finally ordered them to cross-reference the inscription locations against the current mine shaft maps, his voice was level, but his hands remained flat against the table as though he needed to feel the stone beneath him. Elsewhere in the garrison, Kessa Stonebraid's fever spiked at dawn, and the physician noted with unease that the skin near the original glyph wound had taken on the faint, unmistakable sheen of mineral — not illness, not bruising, but something closer to the quality of polished rock. Elder Ashveil, excluded from the archive session and aware of her exclusion, spent the morning alone in her quarters writing a document she had not yet decided how to use. When the Seer passed her door at midmorning she opened it and asked him whether the name given to a thing changed what the thing was, or only how it could be addressed. He sat with the question for a long time before answering that he believed naming gave a thing shape it had previously lacked — not more power, but more presence. He had already noted in his journal that the cold pull emanating from below the garrison now had direction: downward and slightly east, toward the sealed western gallery, toward the oldest stone, toward whatever had been listening since before Lostfeld existed.

Far to the north, in the grey hours before full light, Rowen Blacktide stood alone over his extended charts in the stern cabin of his flagship and wrote three words in his private log: *the triangle closes.* Navigator Briggs had confirmed it — the third map point buried in the far margin of the chart, encoded in the same hand as the two already known, placed the convergence zone beyond the northern current boundary in waters that winter would seal within two days. Briggs had given him his answer and been sent back to the navigation deck without further discussion. Rowen spent the remainder of the afternoon with his pipe, watching the two Tidefall observation vessels through his stern glass, calculating not the destination, which he was certain of, but the cost. Moving north meant moving beyond the range of those ships. It meant committing to something that could not be easily undone. He was not yet ready to give the order, but the stillness of a man who has already made his decision in everything but word had settled over him, and the Tidefall vessels that watched him from a cautious distance could not have known how little time remained before the water chose for him.

In Regency Hall, Caeris Thorne set the Farrock emergency dispatch beside the Tidefall condition letter and looked at them together for a long moment before sending his steward to delay anyone who might interrupt him. The dispatch confirmed what rumor had only suggested: Lostfeld sealed, the Monastery delegation inside, the Seer last known at the garrison, Gnome pass traffic elevated at both northern access points. He summoned Lord Halveth and the High Cleric for midday and told neither what the other had been shown. Halveth advised nominal acceptance of Tidefall's military suspension demand, the borderlands defined narrowly enough to preserve operational flexibility without signaling weakness. The High Cleric asked, with particular care, whether the Seer's presence at Lostfeld was officially known. Caeris confirmed it was rumor only. He authorized Halveth to draft the Tidefall reply and sent a private inquiry northward to Farrock requesting further intelligence, framing his inaction as prudence, his delay as gathering, and keeping to himself the particular quality of stillness that belongs to a man who understands that two pressures arriving on the same morning are not a coincidence but a convergence — and that convergences, in Aeloria, do not resolve themselves in favor of those who hesitate longest.
