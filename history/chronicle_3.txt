# The Month of the Iron Thaw, Year 1 of the Hollow Accord

The first blood of open war was not heralded by trumpets or the thunder of massed armies. It came quietly, the way most irreversible things do — in the green dark of Greymere Hollow, where Clan Arkenor had spent three patient days building a silence that looked exactly like nothing. Eleven Wayfarers went into that hollow as scouts. None walked out. Three were taken, and whatever use was made of them in the hours that followed was not recorded in any ledger that would survive the season. Sovereign Elowen Silverleaf received the count at dawn, standing alone at the edge of the Faerwood tree-line with her remaining war-bands massed behind her, and she said nothing. She recalled them to the wood without explanation, sent no replacements, issued no orders for six full days. It was on the seventh that she dispatched a single messenger northward, toward Frostvale — carrying no written word, only a Wayfarer death-braid woven tight at both ends, the kind a sovereign makes only when she has chosen to burn something she once loved. The Glenhaven elves did not break. They obeyed her silence as absolutely as they would have obeyed a war-cry, which told the world everything about what was coming and precisely nothing about when.

Far beneath the surface, in the carved dark of Lostfeld, something older than patience had finally found its leverage. Kessa Stonebraid had passed a single name through the crack in the lower stone — one word, written in a steady hand — and Thane Ironmaul had read it, gone the color of old ash, and fed the paper to a candle before the ink had fully dried. By morning he had ordered the antechamber unsealed, an act his loyalists received the way soldiers receive the news of a retreat: not with anger, but with the hollow quiet of men who have just learned the ground beneath them is softer than they thought. Kessa emerged unharmed and eerily composed, her sleeves pushed to the elbow to reveal a procession of symbols she had drawn across her forearms in some pigment that did not quite match any ink known to the deep clans. She refused to repeat the name. She refused to explain the symbols. In the tunnels below the Thane's hall, the great pulse that had beaten its 43-count rhythm for longer than any dwarf could remember had shifted — 44 beats now, with the pause between cycles cut nearly in half, as though whatever was counting down had grown impatient with the pace of the world above it.

In the Twin Cities, theater had replaced governance with such completeness that the court itself no longer seemed certain which performance it was attending. King Roderic Thorne II had appeared before the full assembly — standing without aid, his eyes clear and deliberate — and dissolved Prince Aldric's compact with Tidefall in a single spoken sentence, the way one snuffs a candle one has decided one no longer requires. Aldric did not flinch. He did not protest. He kept his face arranged in an expression of dignified acceptance for the better part of an hour, and that evening an unmarked Tidefall skiff was seen sliding into the Eldoria private dock under a moonless sky, where Aldric met someone aboard for two hours and kept no record of it. By the following morning, Admiral Electorate had moved three additional Tidefall warships into Eldoria harbor, citing as justification the very compact Roderic had just declared void — smiling, it was said, in the manner of a man who has already been paid for goods the buyer thinks are still in negotiation. To the south, beneath a warm wind that had not blown through Frostvale in eleven years, Prince Caeris Thorne sat before the mountain council under a borrowed name, his Gloomspire gnome escort already gone — vanished into the high passes the same night they delivered him, as though their purpose had ended precisely there, and whatever came next was not meant to involve witnesses.
