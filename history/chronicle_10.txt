# The Tenth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

On the tenth day of the Ashen Dreaming, the world did not break — it bent, in two places at once, along lines that had been weakening for longer than most living things had names. At midday, beneath the pale iron light of a sun that offered warmth to no one, the Seer passed through the gates of the Twin Cities and was escorted without ceremony into Caeris Thorne's emergency council — already in session, already fracturing at its seams. He spoke briefly. He did not perform. Whatever words left his mouth moved through the assembled lords like a current through standing water, disturbing everything without touching anything that could be held as evidence. No two men in that chamber would later tell the same story of what was said, and yet every man in that chamber would remember, with perfect clarity, the color that left Caeris Thorne's face. He dismissed the session immediately. He retrieved the regency proclamation from beneath the floorboard where it had hidden like a guilty thing. He did not sign it. He stood alone in the inner chamber holding parchment in steady hands with a face that had forgotten steadiness entirely. In the gallery above, a Gnome agent of the Gloomspire Syndicate recorded what no transcript would — the exact architecture of a powerful man receiving news he had prayed would not arrive.

Far to the east, in the same hour that the Seer rested his voice in a side chamber and declined to eat for the third day running, Pirate King Rowen descended through cold salt water to the iron door at low tide. He brought four men. The key turned. The door opened inward into darkness sustained by air that had no business being there — an air pocket of unknown origin sealed behind iron beneath the waves of a sinking island, waiting. Twenty minutes passed. Rowen surfaced alone. He gave no explanation for where his four companions had gone, and his face offered nothing that could be called reassurance. He ordered the fleet northeast before the water had finished running from his hair. By nightfall the iron door was beyond reaching, swallowed again by a sea that had merely permitted the intrusion rather than invited it, and Rowen sat sealed in his cabin with whatever he had carried out of that underwater dark, refusing even his captains the shape of it. His silence had a texture — purposeful and heavy, the silence of a man trying to determine whether what he was holding was a weapon or a wound.

And below the Lostfeld mountains, before dawn had reached any of them — before the Seer had crossed any threshold and before Rowen had drawn breath beneath the waves — the freshly bricked gate had moved a single stone outward. No violence. No sound beyond the sound of mortar accepting what it could not hold. The hammering that had filled the garrison's nights for eight days was gone, replaced by a quiet that soldiers described, in whispers, as the quality of listening. In the upper halls, Kessa Stonebraid sat upright and spoke a complete sentence in a language no living mouth was meant to hold, and then sat still again with her palms pressed to her thighs, symbols deepened in the skin, breathing with the rhythm of someone receiving instruction from a very long distance. The guards who heard it could not repeat it. One wept without knowing why. When Thane Ironmaul read the Grand Druid's warning — delivered by a rider whose hands had not yet stopped shaking — he read it holding the displaced stone, already cold, already proof that warnings were a courtesy the deep had decided to dispense with. He sent the rider back with seven words: *Come in person. Bring what closes doors.* Across the world, the Grand Druid Varek was already packing. The Conclave was already rising. The distance between Lostfeld and everything that might help it was exactly as long as it needed to be to ensure that nothing arrived in time.
