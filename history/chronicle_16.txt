# The Sixteenth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

Before midday, in the stone-cold interior of the Lostfeld garrison, Thane Ironmaul asked his question only once. He stood over the iron case where it rested on the floor like a wound that had closed wrong, and he looked at Varek — at whatever Varek had become behind those altered eyes — and he said: *Did you open it?* The Grand Druid did not answer immediately. He looked at the case for a long time, the way a man looks at something that has already looked back at him, and then he said, quietly and without apology: *It opened for me.* Ironmaul did not ask again. There was nothing left to ask. He ordered the outer gate sealed before the afternoon shadows had found their length, and he sat beside the iron case through the hours that followed without touching it, without speaking, a thane keeping watch over a thing he could not name and could not abandon. Varek ate but did not sleep. His eyes, still wrong, still carrying some residue of whatever had passed in the deep place below, remained fixed on the case as though conversation between them continued in a register no other living creature could hear.

At dusk, the Monastery instruction rider arrived at Lostfeld's outer gate and found it sealed from within. He called through the gate slot and a guard answered in flat, practiced tones: no visitors, by order of the Thane. The rider camped at the road's edge in the cold and sent his message back toward Stonebreak before the last light failed — *gate sealed, Varek's status unknown, awaiting instruction* — not knowing that at Stonebreak the council had already moved past instruction. Elder Sondra Ashveil had been named and authorized before the rider's horse had reached its camp, her dispensation full and her authority total: to assess, to act, and if necessary, to contain. She would depart at dawn. Meanwhile, a Vilefin tinker who had spent days posing as a common craftsman packed his tools in the dark and slipped away down the southern road, carrying in his memory a package of intelligence worth more than iron: the supply silence, the extra garrison posts, the unfinished iron frame standing cold and purposeless in the yard, and the sight of the Monastery's own rider turned away at the gate like a common vagrant. By morning, that knowledge would be in motion toward every faction in Aeloria willing to pay for the shape of another faction's fear.

Far to the south, in the Twin Cities, a different kind of silence was being maintained with greater effort. Before dawn, a palace physician had confirmed to the attending steward that King Roderic Thorne II had pressed his seal — those aged, uncertain hands pressing down one final time on something that would outlast him — and by breakfast the document had been carried by Caeris himself to the palace archivist, where it became law without fanfare, without announcement, without the world knowing it had changed. Caeris visited his father again in the morning and found Roderic asleep, unreachable even to the son who now held his succession in a sealed scroll. By midmorning the garrison captain had brought worse news: a Dur Khadur advance unit camped four miles from the harbor approach, a foreign force sitting on the city's threshold with no explanation the city could understand, because the Admiral of Tidefall — who had whispered that force into motion — remained sealed behind his study door for a second full day, his prophet's notes unanswered, his council meeting without him for the first time in Tidefall's memory. Caeris did not flinch. He ordered the garrison to elevated readiness but not to move, and he sent a letter to the Admiral's office in formal language asking for clarification, buying hours he did not know he had. Near midnight, the Shadow Court envoy had found him in his private anteroom and offered recognition, and Caeris had listened with the patience of a man who has already won the thing being offered, and said nothing that closed a door. The proclamation was real. The heir was legal. The world simply had not been told yet — and in the space between the truth and its announcement, every faction in Aeloria was moving blind, selling what they knew and buying what they feared, while above it all the iron case sat watched and unsealed and full of whatever the deep thing had chosen to leave behind.
