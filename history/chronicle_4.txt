# The Month of the Amber Silence

The world held its breath that month, though no one called it silence by choice. In the deep forests of Faerwood, a messenger returned from the northern mountain of Frostvale bearing something small enough to hide in a closed fist and heavy enough to redraw every alliance in Aeloria — Caeris Thorne's personal signet ring, pressed into the palm of Sovereign Elowen Silverleaf like a coal that had not yet decided whether to burn. She said nothing to her war-council. She denied nothing either. But within days the war-bands that had long watched the Faerwood border were quietly, methodically redeployed — not south, not east, but northward along the high ridge paths toward Frostvale, moving through amber-shadowed pine and frozen stone as if Elowen had already made a decision she had not yet admitted to herself. Above the forest canopy, the twin moon-shadow that had long split the night sky fused back into one — and then drifted, slow and purposeless, unmoored from the moon that ought to have cast it. Three nights running, Clan Arkenor barred their doors and did not say why.

In Lostfeld, the question of silence had become monstrous in a different way. The pulse beneath the mountain — that rhythmic, geological heartbeat the dwarves had learned to measure and fear — had ceased to pulse at all. It no longer came and went in counted intervals. It was now a sustained vibration, continuous and low, felt not in the ears but in the teeth, in the marrow, in the mortar between stones that had stood for four hundred years. Kessa Stonebraid was found at dawn standing at the sealed mine-gate below the antechamber, her palm flat against the rock face, the symbols that had begun on her forearms now crawling up her throat in faint, cold light. She could not be woken. When she finally opened her eyes, hours later, her first words were a question — *has it opened yet* — spoken with the calm of someone who already knew the answer and was simply keeping count. A blind lore-keeper in Stonebreak, shown sketches of Kessa's markings, went very still and said that three of the symbols were identical to those the novice had been etching into the walls of the Sealed Garden. The novice, for his part, no longer spoke. He mapped. He slept twelve hours, woke for two, and mapped again, covering the warm interior walls of the Garden floor to ceiling while the Grand Druid stopped eating and the Conclave dispersed without consensus, having agreed on nothing except that whatever was happening could not be stopped by committee.

In the Twin Cities, indecision wore the crown and everyone at court could see it. King Roderic Thorne II had drafted an ultimatum to Tidefall — thirty days for their three warships to vacate Eldoria harbor — and then set down his pen without signing it, leaving the document to exist as threat, as bait, as a mirror held up to see who would flinch first. Prince Aldric Thorne learned of the draft through a court clerk and showed no visible reaction, which was itself the reaction of a man who had been waiting for exactly this. That evening he sent a coded courier northward — not toward Tidefall, but toward Frostvale — the letter sealed with a glyph that bore no Thorne mark whatsoever, but was recognized by those who knew such things as the cipher of the Dur Khadur Dark Council. A flock of starlings turned above Eldoria palace that same week and held, impossibly, the shape of a signet ring for six full minutes before breaking apart like a sentence interrupted mid-word. The court astrologer resigned before sundown and offered no explanation. To the west, in the waters off the Dreadwind Islands, Pirate King Rowen launched two ships under no flag and remained at home himself, publicly visible, wearing plausible deniability like a second coat. The ships carried no soldiers and no cargo — only instruments for measuring the depth of the sea floor. Someone was mapping what lay beneath the Tidefall shipping lanes, and the vanished commander of Clan Arkenor was confirmed aboard the lead vessel, sailing toward water that did not yet know it was being charted.
