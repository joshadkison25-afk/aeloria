The twenty-first day of the Month of the Ashen Dreaming broke without mercy across Aeloria, as if the world had decided that patience was a luxury it could no longer afford. In the sealed garrison at Lostfeld, Kessa Stonebraid found the fourth etching at dawn — a single connecting line drawn between the three spirals on the iron case, as if some slow and patient hand had reached through the dark hours to finish a thought. Grand Druid Varek had slept the entire night without sound or movement for the first time since his arrival, and the garrison soldiers who had grown accustomed to his nocturnal murmuring found the silence worse than anything that had preceded it. Elder Ashveil stood before the case in the grey morning light, said nothing to Thane Ironmaul, and later approached Varek in the yard to ask what the figure wished to complete. He did not answer in words. He knelt and drew a circle in the dirt with one finger, slowly, and left it there. Far to the south on the mountain road out of Stonebreak, the Seer rode northward on a Monastery supply cart, carrying a vision he could not name — a woman's hands on cold iron, a bloodline marked by something older than any curse he had learned to read — and he was three days away from the woman he needed to warn, and the case was not waiting for him.

In Twin Cities, Caeris Thorne moved through the morning like a man holding a dozen threads and feeling each one fray in turn. Lady Orryn had come in person to give her answer, which was itself a statement, and the answer she gave was conditional — she would sign the proclamation, but with a clause permitting her withdrawal within ten days should King Roderic Thorne II recover. Caeris had thirty minutes to decide and he accepted, because he had no other choice, and because the amended text now had to return to the High Cleric for re-review, adding twelve hours to a timeline that had already grown tight enough to cut. He sent a holding message to Lord Halveth, composed a careful and deliberately vague reply to Farrock's inquiry about the northern corridor, and watched from his window as two Glenhaven Wayfarers took up their quiet observer positions in the street below — allies in form, witnesses in practice. Every hour before the proclamation was formally announced was an hour in which something could go wrong, and Caeris Thorne knew this with the particular exhaustion of a man who has spent weeks preventing catastrophe and cannot yet see the shore.

At the admiralty hall in Tidefall, the council chair arrived at dawn with two witnesses to exercise the incapacitation protocol — and found the door already open. Admiral sat at his desk in full dress, the Dur Khadur dispatch and the Hollow Accord letter open before him, read. He looked up at the chair and said only: *Sit.* What passed between them in the hour that followed was heard by no one — not the Vampire operative who lingered in the corridor, not the prophet who waited outside the hall for three full hours before departing unreceived, his face unreadable. The chair emerged alone at midday and called an emergency closed council session, and the city of Tidefall received no statement, no explanation, no word of what the Admiral had concluded from the documents that had been kept from him, or what he intended to do with the authority that had never, in truth, entirely left his hands.
