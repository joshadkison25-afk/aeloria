# The Thirteenth Day of the Ashen Dreaming

At the ninth hour of the thirteenth day, the sun above the Twin Cities ceased to exist. There was no warning in the clouds, for there were no clouds. There was no shift in the wind, for the air hung perfectly still. The light simply went, as a candle goes when a hand cups it — completely, and with the quiet authority of something that has decided. For eleven minutes the Twin Cities stood in a darkness that belonged to no natural hour, and in that darkness every soul within them became briefly legible. Merchants stopped mid-sentence with their hands still raised. Soldiers on the palace walls looked upward the way men look at something falling toward them. In the courtyard below, the Seer stood with his arms at his sides and his face tilted toward the extinguished sun and did not flinch, did not pray aloud, did not move at all — as though he had been expecting this, or as though he understood that the only honest response to such a thing was stillness. Caeris Thorne stood at the open window of the east wing, the proclamation document waiting on his desk behind him like an unanswered question, and he watched the darkness hold the city in its palm for those eleven minutes and did not move until the light returned, slow and almost apologetic, as if the sun itself was uncertain of its welcome.

Far to the north, in the mountain dark beneath Lostfeld, Grand Druid Varek descended into the sealed level with his iron case and spoke no words to the guards who brought him to the threshold. He did not know, as he passed through that door into whatever waited below, that in the same hour the sky above the Twin Cities had swallowed itself whole. The guards at the threshold heard nothing through the door. Kessa Stonebraid remained motionless in the lower chamber, as she had remained for days, a woman become a landmark of something wrong. Above them all, Thane Ironmaul received the eclipse report from a surface watch and sat down heavily in his chair and did not rise for twenty minutes — a stillness that his garrison noticed the way soldiers always notice when the man who holds the wall stops moving. When he finally stood, he tripled the watch on the fracture point and wrote to the Thane Council in plain, stripped language that broke from every careful word he had used before: *The druid is below. The sun went dark above the Twin Cities today. I do not know if these things are connected. I know I cannot pretend otherwise.*

By midday the eclipse was moving across the world in the form of rider dispatches and whispered reports, and every faction it reached bent it immediately into the shape of its own fear or hunger. In Faerwood, Lythara of the Shadow Court stopped writing mid-sentence and convened her inner circle and declared the eclipse a mandate written in the old tradition: a sun-eating above a city means a hidden throne is preparing to become visible before the dark moon, and she believed — with the absolute certainty of someone who has been waiting for permission — that she was the one meant to sit in it. In the halls of the Gloomspire Syndicate, the calculus was colder but no less urgent; the eclipse had created a buyer's fear premium, and they dispatched a sealed offer to Caeris Thorne's private secretary that sold not merely intelligence but urgency itself. And in a wavering lord's chamber in the palace district, a Vampire operative recognized the eclipse for exactly what it was to his purposes — eleven minutes of spiritual openness, a window of psychological nakedness — and pressed the shaken man for the final inference he had withheld for two days, and received it. The Seer, when the light returned, walked without haste to the palace scriptorium and wrote for an hour without stopping, setting aside the careful note he had been composing for days and beginning instead something longer and more exposed — a document addressed to no one yet, which named the eclipse and the garrison order and the dream-reports filtering down from the north and the shape of what he believed was assembling beneath the surface of the world simultaneously, like roots converging beneath a field that has not yet begun to burn.
